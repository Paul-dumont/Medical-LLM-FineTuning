LOUISE SYMINGTON								10/29/2008

Lou is 57 years old, referred by Darlene Robertson of Diamond Springs Drive, Houston, TX 77077.  

HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS:  Lou’s TMJ problems began of unknown etiology at about the age of 31 in 1983.  She subsequently saw Dr. Ray Reid.  She has then had the following surgeries:

In 1983, bilateral TMJ arthroplasty with disc repositioning.  She subsequently has had multiple steroid injections in and around the joints for many years following including up to the last few months.  She has had extensive physical therapy through this time also.
In 1987, bilateral TMJ Vitek-Kent total joint prostheses were placed.
In 1989, the mandibular component of the Vitek-Kent total joint prostheses were removed bilaterally and replaced with Synthes mandibular components.
In 1994, she had bilateral TMJ debridement with apparently some removal of heterotopic bone.
In 1996, the previously placed total joint prostheses were removed, the joint debrided, and the bone of mandibular joints were reconstructed with Christiansen total joint prostheses with acrylic heads. 

Lou rates her TMJ pain at about a level of 9, average daily pain at 9, jaw function at 10, diet at 6, and disability at 8.  She gets headaches in the frontal, temporal, posterior head and on top of the head bilaterally at about a level of 8.  She has no problems with earaches, but does get occasional tinnitus and occasional vertigo.  No other joints bother her.  She is aware of clenching at nighttime and also during the day.  

Lou takes the following medications:

Divigel (estrogen replacement).
Prometrium (progesterone replacement).
Vicodin 5-mg tablets 1 tablet q.4 to 6 hours with an average of 4 per day.
Aleve.
Calcium.
Aspirin 81 mg daily.
Lexapro 20 mg q.d.

Primary concerns that Lou has at this time include the following:

Pain. 
Limited opening. 
Disruption of normal life activities. 

In addition, Lou does have snoring problems and she does have some daytime tiredness as well. She has difficulty sleeping primarily related to positional changes that cause pain for her.  She generally tries to sleep on her stomach with her head propped on her hands to keep the sides of the jaws from receiving any pressure.

Left commissure is down about 3 to 4 mm compared to the right side.  In profile view, the maxilla appears retruded by about 4 to 6 mm.  Chin appears retruded by about 15 mm. Likewise in the upper arch, she has all of her teeth back through the second molars.  She has basically a Class I cuspid-molar relationship just with a slight Class II tendency on the left side. She has about a 1 mm vertical overbite relationship.  To palpation, she has relatively severe pain to palpation over the masseter muscles, medial pterygoids, TMJs, and the retromandibular areas.  She has high moderate pain over the posterior neck and shoulder areas and moderate pain over the temporalis and SCMs.  Intranasally, turbinates look relatively normal in size as best as they could be observed.  The septum is deviated just slightly off to the right side.  
RADIOGRAPHIC EVALUATION.  Panorex shows the presence of bilateral Christiansen prostheses with acrylic heads.  Sinuses look relatively clear, although there could be a little something in the right sinus.  She has root canals on the maxillary right second bicuspids, second molar, and left second bicuspid.  All four third molars are absent.  
Waters view shows that the sinuses appear relatively clear.  Bony nasal septum is reasonably straight.  Turbinates appear moderate in size.  She has bilateral Christiansen prostheses present and it appears that there is metal-on-metal contact.  There appear to be about 3 to 4 screws stabilizing each of the fossa components and probably 6 screws in each side supporting the mandibular portion of the prosthesis. 
Left TMJ tomogram shows that it appears she has had a previous coronoidectomy.  There appears to be some heterotopic bone around the prosthesis particularly anterior.  It appears that there is metal-on-metal articulation of the mandibular component with fossa component. 
Right TMJ tomogram shows there may be some heterotopic bone surrounding the joint.  The Christiansen prosthesis is present and there may be metal-on-metal contact.  On opening, there is virtually no movement at all in the joint.  
Lateral cephalometric radiograph shows that Lou has a retruded maxilla and mandible.  The oropharyngeal airway is difficult to assess, but likely has some fairly severe constricture.  There is a moderate high occlusal plane angle.  Bilateral joint prostheses and metal fossas are evident.

Basic diagnoses for Louise would be as follows:    

Bilateral TMJ failed Christiansen total joint prosthesis.
Heterotopic bone formation and foreign body giant cell reaction around the failed Christiansen total joint prosthesis.
Bilateral TMJ and mandibular bony ankylosis.
AP maxillary hypoplasia and posterior vertical maxillary hypoplasia.
AP mandibular hypoplasia.  
Bilateral TMJ bony anklyosis. 
Decreased oropharyngeal airway. 
Sleep apnea symptoms.
Severe TMJ pain. 
Headaches.
Myofascial pain. 

Recommended treatment would be as follows:

CT scan of jaws and jaw joints.
Consider sleep apnea study. 
Surgery.
Bilateral removal of failed Christiansen total joint prosthesis.
Thorough debridement with removal of heterotopic bone and foreign body giant cell reaction. 
Bilateral TMJ reconstruction and mandibular advancement with TMJ Concepts total joint prostheses.
Bilateral TMJ fat grafts (harvest from the abdomen). 
Maxillary osteotomies to advance it and down graft the posterior aspect.
